Preliminary Concepts (Read Once)

Your phone's keyboard predicts the next word as you type — "I am going to the …" → it suggests "office", "market", "movie". How does it know? It has learned, from millions of sentences, which word USUALLY comes next given the previous few words. That is a LANGUAGE MODEL — a mathematical model that assigns probabilities to sequences of words.

1. What is Language Modelling?

A language model is a probability distribution over sequences of words. It tells us how likely a particular sentence is in a given language. Given some past context, it can predict the next word.

2. Types of language models

  • Statistical / Probabilistic models — Markov models, n-gram models.
  • Log-linear / Maximum entropy models — features-based.
  • Neural language models — RNNs, LSTMs, Transformers (BERT, GPT).
  • Graph-based models — encode word relationships as graphs.
  • Topic models — LSA, LDA, NMF (for document-level patterns).

3. Word representations (Word Embeddings)

  • Bag-of-words (BoW) — counts of each word.
  • TF-IDF — weighted by importance across documents.
  • word2vec / GloVe — dense vector embeddings.
  • BERT / contextualized — word vectors depend on context.

Q1 — N-gram / Bigram Models with Smoothing (Numerical)

Asked 4 times(May'23, Nov'23, May'24, Nov'25 — usually 8–9 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'23 & May'24, 8 marks) Given a corpus of 10,000 words, vocab 5000, bigram "the cat" appears 50 times, unigram "the" 1000 times, "cat" 100 times. Using add-k smoothing with k=0.5, find probability of "the cat sat on the mat".

(Nov'23, 8 marks) Given a small corpus, find Bigram probability of a test sentence.

(Nov'25, 9 marks) Construct bigram model from corpus; compute smoothed bigram probability of a sentence using Add-1 smoothing; show all steps.

Answer
A bigram model is like watching ONE step back in a conversation: to guess the next word, look only at the immediately previous word. Example: P("cat" | "the") = "of all the times 'the' appeared, what fraction was it followed by 'cat'?"

1. What is an N-gram model?

An N-gram is a sequence of N consecutive words. An n-gram language model approximates the probability of the next word given the previous (n−1) words using counts from a text corpus.

  • Unigram (n=1): P(w) — based only on word's own frequency.
  • Bigram (n=2): P(wi | wi-1) — depends on previous 1 word.
  • Trigram (n=3): P(wi | wi-2, wi-1) — depends on previous 2 words.

2. Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE) formula

P(wi | wi-1) = Count(wi-1, wi) / Count(wi-1)

3. The need for smoothing

  • If a bigram never appears in the corpus, its MLE probability = 0.
  • One zero kills the whole sentence probability (since we multiply).
  • SMOOTHING redistributes some probability mass to unseen bigrams.

4. Add-k (Lidstone / Laplace) Smoothing

Padd-k(wi | wi-1) = (Count(wi-1, wi) + k) / (Count(wi-1) + k · V)

where V = vocabulary size and k is a small constant (k = 1 for Laplace; k = 0.5 for add-half).

5. SOLVED EXAMPLE (May'23 question)

Given: Corpus = 10,000 words; V = 5000; Count("the cat") = 50; Count("the") = 1000; Count("cat") = 100. k = 0.5.

Sentence: "the cat sat on the mat" → bigrams = (the, cat), (cat, sat), (sat, on), (on, the), (the, mat)

Apply add-0.5 smoothing for each bigram:

P("cat" | "the") = (50 + 0.5) / (1000 + 0.5 × 5000)
                 = 50.5 / 3500
                 ≈ 0.01443

For bigrams that don't appear in the corpus (e.g. cat-sat, sat-on, on-the, the-mat):
Suppose Count = 0 and the unigram preceding it is in corpus.

P("sat" | "cat") = (0 + 0.5) / (Count("cat") + 0.5 × V)
                 = 0.5 / (100 + 2500)
                 = 0.5 / 2600
                 ≈ 0.000192

For P("on" | "sat"), P("the" | "on"), P("mat" | "the"):
since unigram counts aren't given, assume Count(w_{i-1}) = small and similar pattern.
For simplicity, assume each unseen-bigram probability ≈ 0.5 / (Count + 0.5·V).

Sentence probability = product of all 5 bigram probabilities.

(In an exam, show the formula application clearly for each bigram and multiply at the end. Even an approximate final answer is acceptable if working is shown.)

6. Other smoothing techniques

  • Good-Turing smoothing — uses count-of-counts.
  • Kneser-Ney smoothing — most popular for n-gram LMs.
  • Interpolation — combine unigram, bigram, trigram with weights.
  • Backoff — fall back to lower n-gram if higher one has count 0.

7. Evaluation of language models

  • Perplexity: 2−log P(sentence). Lower = better.
  • Cross-entropy — average log-loss per word.
  • Extrinsic: test on actual downstream task (speech recognition accuracy, etc.).

Key points to remember

  • n-gram = sequence of n consecutive words.
  • Bigram MLE: P(wi|wi-1) = Count(wi-1, wi) / Count(wi-1).
  • Smoothing avoids zero probabilities.
  • Add-k: numerator +k, denominator +k·V.
  • Sentence probability = product of all bigram probabilities.
  • Other smoothings: Good-Turing, Kneser-Ney, interpolation, backoff.

Q2 — TF-IDF (Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency)

Asked 4 times(May'23, Nov'23, May'25, Nov'25 — usually 4–9 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'23 & Nov'23, 6 marks) Given a document-term matrix, calculate TF-IDF score of Term 1 in Document 1.

(May'25, 4 marks) Write a short note on TF-IDF representation.

(Nov'25, 9 marks) Given 3 documents, compute TF-IDF of term "neural"; show DF, IDF; interpret.

Answer
Imagine you're searching for the most important word in a magazine article. Common words like "the", "is", "of" appear everywhere — they're not informative. But a word like "supernova" appearing several times in ONE article and not in others tells you that article is probably about astronomy. TF-IDF formalizes this idea: a word's importance grows with its frequency in a document but is reduced if it appears in many documents.

1. Definition

TF-IDF = Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency. It is a numerical statistic that measures how IMPORTANT a word is to a particular document in a collection of documents.

2. Components

(a) Term Frequency (TF)

TF(t, d) = (Number of times t appears in d) / (Total terms in d)

Or simply the raw count: TF(t, d) = count(t in d).

(b) Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)

IDF(t) = log( N / DF(t) )

where N = total documents, DF(t) = number of documents containing t.

(c) TF-IDF

TF-IDF(t, d) = TF(t, d) × IDF(t)

3. SOLVED EXAMPLE (May'23 question)

Document-Term Matrix:

Document 1Document 2Document 3
Term 11050
Term 2208
Term 3136

Step 1: TF(Term 1, Doc 1) = 10 (raw count) or 10/13 (if normalized by document length).

Step 2: DF(Term 1) = number of documents containing Term 1 = 2 (Doc 1 and Doc 2; not Doc 3).

Step 3: N = 3 documents. IDF(Term 1) = log(3 / 2) ≈ 0.176 (natural log) or ≈ 0.405 (log base 2).

Step 4: TF-IDF(Term 1, Doc 1) = 10 × 0.176 = 1.76 (using natural log) or 10 × 0.405 = 4.05 (using log base 2).

4. Intuition

  • If a word appears in EVERY document → DF = N → IDF = log(1) = 0 → TF-IDF = 0. (Common words → ignored.)
  • If a word appears in only 1 document → DF = 1 → IDF = log(N) (high) → TF-IDF is high.
  • Higher TF-IDF = more distinctive word for that document.

5. Applications

  • Search engines — rank documents by relevance to query.
  • Document classification — used as features.
  • Keyword extraction — top TF-IDF words are likely the topic.
  • Information retrieval — basic component of Vector Space Model.
  • Text similarity — cosine similarity over TF-IDF vectors.

6. Advantages and limitations

  • + Simple, interpretable, no training needed.
  • + Down-weights common words like "the", "is".
  • − No understanding of semantics (synonyms treated as different).
  • − Sparse high-dimensional vectors.
  • − Doesn't capture word ORDER.

Key points to remember

  • TF-IDF = TF(t, d) × IDF(t).
  • IDF = log(N / DF) — common words get low IDF.
  • High TF-IDF = word is FREQUENT in d but RARE in corpus.
  • Used in search engines, document classification, keyword extraction.
  • Limitation: ignores semantics and word order.

Q3 — Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA)

Asked 3 times(May'23, Nov'23, May'25 — usually 4–8 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'23, 4 marks) Write a short note on Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA).

(Nov'23, 6 marks) Explain in detail Latent Semantic Analysis for topic modelling.

(May'25, 8 marks) Explain the concept of LSA and how it is used for identifying relationships between words.

Answer
Two articles about "car" and "automobile" should be treated as similar — they discuss the same topic with different words. Word-by-word matching fails here. LSA helps by finding hidden "concept dimensions" (topics) underlying the documents, so that synonyms and related words get placed close together in the topic space.

1. Definition

Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) is a dimensionality-reduction technique used in NLP that uses Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on a document-term matrix to uncover hidden "latent" semantic relationships between words and documents.

2. The motivation

  • TF-IDF treats different words as completely independent.
  • But words like "car" and "automobile" mean almost the same thing.
  • LSA finds latent topics by grouping words that often appear together.

3. Steps of LSA

  1. Build the term-document matrix A: rows = terms, columns = documents, cells = counts or TF-IDF.
  2. Apply Singular Value Decomposition (SVD):
    A = U · Σ · VT
  3. Keep only the top k singular values (largest values in Σ). This compresses the matrix to k latent dimensions ("topics").
  4. Reconstruct lower-rank approximation: Ak = Uk · Σk · VkT.
  5. Each row of Uk = vector for a TERM in latent space.
  6. Each column of VkT = vector for a DOCUMENT in latent space.
  7. Similarity between two terms/docs computed using cosine similarity in this latent space.

4. Diagram (text)

                   Documents
       Original    →    →    →
       Term-Doc    ┌───────────┐         ┌───┐ ┌───┐ ┌──────┐
       Matrix A =  │           │  ≈   U  │   │·│ Σ │·│ V^T  │
                   │ (m × n)   │   m×k   k×k       k×n
                   └───────────┘
                                  (k latent topics, k << min(m,n))

5. How LSA identifies relationships

  • Words that occur in similar documents end up close together in the k-dimensional latent space.
  • Synonyms like "car" / "automobile" become similar vectors.
  • Documents about similar topics cluster together.
  • A query can be projected into the same space; we retrieve the closest documents.

6. Advantages

  • Handles synonymy (words with similar meaning).
  • Reduces dimensionality from thousands of terms to a few hundred topics.
  • Unsupervised — no labels needed.
  • Faster retrieval than full TF-IDF.

7. Limitations

  • SVD is computationally expensive for very large matrices.
  • k (number of topics) must be chosen manually.
  • Topics may not be interpretable (just mathematical combinations).
  • Doesn't handle polysemy (one word with multiple meanings) well.
  • Static — adding new documents requires re-computation.

8. Applications

  • Information retrieval (LSI = Latent Semantic Indexing).
  • Document clustering and classification.
  • Plagiarism detection.
  • Essay grading.
  • Cross-lingual retrieval.

Key points to remember

  • LSA = SVD applied to term-document matrix to find latent topics.
  • A = U · Σ · VT; keep top k singular values.
  • Handles synonymy; doesn't handle polysemy.
  • Used in IR, document clustering, plagiarism detection.

Q4 — Generative vs Discriminative Models

Asked 3 times(May'23, Nov'23, May'24 — usually 4–9 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'23, 4 marks) What are generative models of language, and how do they differ from discriminative models?

(Nov'23, 4 marks) What are generative models of language? Explain any one model in detail.

(May'24, 9 marks) Generative vs discriminative models; provide example of generative model.

Answer

1. Definitions

  • Generative model learns the JOINT probability P(x, y). Given a class y, it can GENERATE new instances x. Example: HMM, Naive Bayes, n-gram LM, LDA.
  • Discriminative model learns the CONDITIONAL probability P(y | x). Given an x, it predicts the label y. Example: Logistic Regression, SVM, CRF, BERT classifier.

2. Side-by-side comparison

AspectGenerativeDiscriminative
ModelsP(x, y)P(y | x)
Can generate new data?YesNo
Learns fromLabeled and/or unlabeled dataLabeled data only
Examples in NLPHMM, Naive Bayes, LDA, n-gram LM, GPTLogistic Regression, MaxEnt, CRF, BERT classifier
StrengthCan handle missing data, generate samplesUsually higher accuracy on classification
WeaknessMay not perform best for classificationCannot generate new data

3. Example of generative model in NLP: Naive Bayes

  • Used for text classification (spam vs ham, sentiment positive vs negative).
  • Learns P(word | class) for each class.
  • Uses Bayes' theorem: P(class | text) ∝ P(text | class) · P(class).
  • Assumes "naive" independence between words.
  • Despite simplicity, works well for simple text classification.

4. Example of generative model: HMM (Hidden Markov Model)

  • Used for POS tagging, speech recognition.
  • Models hidden states (POS tags) and observed outputs (words).
  • P(words, tags) = product of transition × emission probabilities.

5. Example of generative model: n-gram language model

  • Models P(word | previous n-1 words).
  • Can be used to GENERATE new sentences by sampling.
  • Foundation of older speech recognition and machine translation systems.

6. Example of generative model: GPT (modern)

  • Modern neural generative model.
  • Generates coherent paragraphs of text.
  • Trained on enormous corpora of internet text.

Key points to remember

  • Generative: P(x, y). Examples: HMM, Naive Bayes, LDA, GPT.
  • Discriminative: P(y | x). Examples: Logistic Reg, SVM, CRF, BERT classifier.
  • Generative can generate samples; discriminative cannot.
  • Discriminative usually achieves higher classification accuracy.
  • Generative handles unlabeled data better.

Q5 — Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)

Asked 3 times(May'23, Nov'23, May'24 — usually 8–9 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'23, 8 marks) Describe LDA algorithm and how it is used for topic modeling.

(Nov'23, 8 marks) Describe LDA algorithm and how it is used for topic modeling.

(May'24, 9 marks) Define LDA and explain its use for topic modeling. Discuss key components: topics, documents, word distributions.

Answer
Imagine you have 1000 news articles. You don't know in advance what topics they cover. LDA reads all the articles and automatically discovers, say, 10 hidden TOPICS — like "sports", "politics", "finance", "weather". Each article is described as a MIXTURE of topics (e.g., 70% politics + 30% economy), and each topic is described as a probability distribution over words.

1. Definition

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is a probabilistic generative model that automatically discovers TOPICS in a collection of documents. Each document is modeled as a mixture of topics, and each topic is a probability distribution over words.

2. Key components / Concepts in LDA

  • Documents: the input — text documents in the corpus.
  • Topics: hidden "concepts" (e.g., "sports", "politics") that we want to discover.
  • Word distribution per topic: each topic has a probability distribution over the vocabulary (e.g., "sports" topic = {ball: 0.05, team: 0.04, player: 0.03, …}).
  • Topic distribution per document: each document has a probability distribution over topics (e.g., 60% politics + 40% finance).
  • Dirichlet prior: a probability distribution used to model the topic and word distributions.

3. The generative story (how LDA "thinks" documents are written)

  1. For each document, pick a TOPIC distribution (from a Dirichlet prior).
  2. For each word position in the document:
    • Pick a topic from this document's topic distribution.
    • Pick a word from this topic's word distribution.
  3. This is how LDA imagines documents were "generated".

4. LDA learning algorithm (high level)

  • Given a corpus, we REVERSE the generative process.
  • Goal: find the topic and word distributions that MAXIMIZE the likelihood of the observed documents.
  • Common methods: Gibbs sampling, Variational Inference.
  • Hyperparameters: number of topics K, Dirichlet priors α (topic) and β (word).

5. Output of LDA

  • K topics, each represented by top words.
  • For each document, a topic distribution.

6. Example of LDA output

Topic 1: ball (0.05), team (0.04), player (0.03), goal (0.02), match (0.02)  → "Sports"
Topic 2: government (0.06), policy (0.04), election (0.03), vote (0.02)      → "Politics"
Topic 3: bank (0.05), rupee (0.04), market (0.03), investor (0.02)            → "Finance"

Document 1: 80% Sports + 15% Politics + 5% Finance
Document 2: 70% Finance + 25% Politics + 5% Sports

7. Applications of LDA

  • Topic discovery in large text collections.
  • Document clustering and classification.
  • Recommender systems (e.g., "you read articles about X, you may like Y").
  • Trend analysis on social media.
  • Search engine result clustering.
  • Plagiarism detection.

8. Advantages and limitations

  • + Unsupervised — no labels needed.
  • + Interpretable topics.
  • + Captures soft membership (document can belong to multiple topics).
  • − Need to choose K (number of topics) manually.
  • − Slow on very large corpora.
  • − Ignores word order (bag-of-words assumption).

Key points to remember

  • LDA = probabilistic generative model for topic discovery.
  • Each document = mixture of topics; each topic = distribution over words.
  • Uses Dirichlet prior; learned via Gibbs sampling or Variational Inference.
  • Output: K topics + topic mixture per document.
  • Applications: topic discovery, document clustering, recommendations.

Q6 — Markov Model for Next-Word Prediction

Asked 2 times(May'23, May'25 — usually 6 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'23, 6 marks) Describe the process of building a simple Markov model for predicting the next word in a sentence with the help of example.

(May'25, 6 marks) What is a Markov model, and how is it used to generate language sequences?

Answer

1. Definition

A Markov model in NLP is a probabilistic model that predicts the next word based on the PREVIOUS N words, assuming the Markov property (future depends only on the immediate past, not the whole history).

2. The Markov property

The probability of the NEXT word depends ONLY on the previous N-1 words, not on all earlier words.

  • First-order Markov (= bigram): depends only on 1 previous word.
  • Second-order Markov (= trigram): depends on 2 previous words.

3. Building a simple Markov model (bigram)

  1. Collect a training corpus of sentences.
  2. Tokenize each sentence into words; add <s> (start) and </s> (end) tokens.
  3. Count bigrams: for each adjacent word pair (wi-1, wi), count its occurrences.
  4. Compute probabilities:
    P(wi | wi-1) = Count(wi-1, wi) / Count(wi-1)
  5. Smoothing (Laplace, add-k, Good-Turing) to handle unseen pairs.
  6. Prediction: given previous word w, the next word is argmaxw' P(w' | w).

4. Example

Corpus:
   I love NLP 
   I love food 
   NLP is fun 

Bigram counts:
  (, I) = 2,  (I, love) = 2,  (love, NLP) = 1,
  (love, food) = 1, (, NLP) = 1, (NLP, is) = 1, (is, fun) = 1
  (NLP, ) = 1, (food, ) = 1, (fun, ) = 1

P("love" | "I") = Count(I, love) / Count(I) = 2 / 2 = 1.0
P("NLP" | "love") = 1 / 2 = 0.5
P("food" | "love") = 1 / 2 = 0.5

So given "I", the next word is most likely "love". Given "love", next can be "NLP" or "food" (50/50).

5. Language sequence generation

  1. Start with <s>.
  2. Sample next word using P(w | <s>).
  3. Use this word as new context; sample again.
  4. Continue until </s> or maximum length.
  5. Generated text is grammatically reasonable for short sequences.

6. Applications

  • Predictive text on phones.
  • Speech recognition.
  • POS tagging (Hidden Markov Models).
  • Text generation.
  • Spelling correction.

7. Limitations

  • Limited context window — can't capture long-range dependencies.
  • Sparse data problem — many n-grams have zero counts.
  • Requires smoothing.
  • Bag-of-words feel — no semantic understanding.

Key points to remember

  • Markov model assumes next word depends only on previous (n-1) words.
  • Building: collect corpus → count n-grams → compute conditional probabilities → smooth.
  • Prediction = argmax of P(w | context).
  • Applications: predictive text, speech, POS tagging.
  • Limitation: short context, needs smoothing.

Q7 — BERT and Contextualized Representations

Asked 2 times(Nov'23, May'24 — usually 4–10 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(Nov'23, 4 marks) Write short note on BERT.

(May'24, 10 marks) Contextualized representations (BERT); advantages and disadvantages.

Answer

1. Definition

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) is a pre-trained deep learning language model developed by Google in 2018. It produces CONTEXTUALIZED word embeddings — each word's vector representation depends on the surrounding context in the sentence.

2. Why BERT was a breakthrough

  • Earlier embeddings (word2vec, GloVe) gave each word a SINGLE static vector — "bank" had the same vector in "river bank" and "money bank".
  • BERT gives each word a DIFFERENT vector based on the surrounding words.
  • This solves the polysemy problem.

3. Architecture

  • Based on the Transformer encoder (Vaswani et al., 2017).
  • Multiple stacked Transformer encoder layers (12 in BERT-base, 24 in BERT-large).
  • Self-attention mechanism allows each word to attend to ALL other words bidirectionally.
  • Input: tokenized sentence with special [CLS] and [SEP] tokens.
  • Output: a vector for each token, plus a sentence-level [CLS] vector.

4. Training (pre-training tasks)

  • Masked Language Model (MLM): randomly mask 15% of words; predict them from context. This forces bidirectional understanding.
  • Next Sentence Prediction (NSP): given two sentences, predict whether the second follows the first. Helps with sentence-level tasks.
  • Pre-trained on BookCorpus and English Wikipedia (~3.3 billion words).

5. Fine-tuning

  • After pre-training, BERT can be fine-tuned on specific tasks (classification, NER, Q&A) by adding small task-specific layers on top.
  • Fine-tuning is fast and uses much less labeled data than training from scratch.

6. Use in NLP

  • Text classification, sentiment analysis.
  • Named Entity Recognition.
  • Question answering (SQuAD).
  • Sentence similarity, paraphrase detection.
  • Text summarization.
  • Search engine query understanding (Google Search uses BERT).

7. Advantages

  • Captures context — handles polysemy.
  • Pre-trained on huge corpora → can transfer to many tasks with little data.
  • Bidirectional — looks at both left and right context.
  • State-of-the-art on many NLP benchmarks (at time of release).

8. Disadvantages

  • Computationally expensive — large model, needs GPU.
  • Long input limit (512 tokens).
  • Pre-training requires huge data and compute.
  • Hard to interpret (deep neural).

9. Variants of BERT

  • RoBERTa — better-trained BERT (more data, longer training).
  • DistilBERT — smaller, faster, 95% performance.
  • ALBERT — parameter-shared, smaller.
  • mBERT — multilingual.
  • IndicBERT — Indian languages.

Key points to remember

  • BERT = Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers.
  • Produces context-aware word embeddings.
  • Pre-trained on MLM + NSP, then fine-tuned for tasks.
  • Architecture: stacked Transformer encoders + self-attention.
  • Advantages: contextual, transferable, state-of-the-art.
  • Disadvantages: expensive, 512-token limit, hard to interpret.

Q8 — Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF)

Asked 2 times(May'25, Nov'25 — usually 4–9 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'25, 4 marks) Explain NMF in the context of topic modeling.

(Nov'25, 9 marks) Explain how NMF differs from LDA in topic modeling.

Answer

1. Definition

Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) is a dimensionality-reduction technique that decomposes a non-negative matrix V into two smaller non-negative matrices W and H such that V ≈ W × H. In NLP, this is used for topic modeling.

2. Mathematical formulation

V (m × n) ≈ W (m × k) · H (k × n)
  • V = term-document matrix.
  • W = topic-term matrix (each column = one topic's word distribution).
  • H = topic-document matrix (each column = one document's topic distribution).
  • k = number of topics (chosen by user).
  • All entries must be ≥ 0 (non-negative).

3. Why non-negative?

  • Word counts and frequencies are naturally non-negative.
  • Non-negativity makes the result INTERPRETABLE — components represent additive parts of the data.
  • Avoids cancellation effects (unlike SVD where negative values can subtract).

4. Algorithm

  1. Initialize W and H with random non-negative values.
  2. Iteratively update W and H using multiplicative update rules to minimize ||V − WH||.
  3. Repeat until convergence.
  4. Final W and H give topic-word and topic-document distributions.

5. NMF vs LDA (key differences)

OptimizationMinimize reconstruction errorMaximize log-likelihood

6. Worked example (concept)

Document-Term Matrix (3 docs, 4 terms):
        the   ball  bank  river
Doc1:    2    5     0     1
Doc2:    1    3     0     2
Doc3:    2    0     4     0

NMF with k=2 topics gives:
   W (topic-term):
        "sports topic"  "finance topic"
   the:      0.30           0.20
   ball:     0.70           0.05
   bank:     0.00           0.85
   river:    0.10           0.00

   H (topic-doc):
                   Doc1   Doc2   Doc3
   "sports topic"  0.80   0.70   0.10
   "finance topic" 0.20   0.30   0.90

Interpretation:
   Doc1 is mostly about sports (ball, the).
   Doc3 is mostly about finance (bank).

7. Applications

  • Topic modeling (alternative to LDA).
  • Document clustering.
  • Image processing (face decomposition).
  • Recommendation systems (collaborative filtering).
  • Bioinformatics (gene expression analysis).

Key points to remember

  • NMF: V ≈ W × H with all entries ≥ 0.
  • W = topic-term matrix; H = topic-document matrix.
  • Non-negativity = interpretable, additive parts.
  • NMF vs LDA: NMF is linear algebra, LDA is probabilistic.
  • Applications: topic modeling, clustering, image, recommendations.

Q9 — Log-Linear Models

Asked 1 time(May'25 — 6 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'25, 6 marks) Explain the concept of log-linear models in NLP. How do these models work, and what are some common applications?

Answer

1. Definition

Log-Linear models (also called Maximum Entropy or MaxEnt models) are a class of discriminative models that compute the probability of a class given input features as the exponential of a weighted sum of features, normalized to sum to 1.

2. Formula

P(y | x) = exp( Σ wi · fi(x, y) ) / Z(x)
  • fi(x, y) = feature functions (binary or real-valued).
  • wi = learned weights.
  • Z(x) = normalization constant (partition function).

3. How they work

  1. Define a set of feature functions fi(x, y) that capture useful properties of the (input, output) pair.
  2. Train: find weights wi that maximize the likelihood of training data.
  3. Predict: for a new input x, output the y that maximizes P(y | x).

4. Advantages

  • Can use arbitrary, overlapping features (unlike Naive Bayes which assumes independence).
  • Maximum entropy principle: makes the fewest assumptions consistent with the data.
  • Probabilistic — gives calibrated probabilities.

5. Limitations

  • Feature engineering is required.
  • Z(x) can be expensive to compute for large output spaces.
  • Largely superseded by neural network models today.

6. Applications in NLP

  • Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging — MaxEnt taggers.
  • Named Entity Recognition (NER).
  • Text classification.
  • Statistical machine translation.
  • Conditional Random Fields (CRF) — a generalization for sequences.

Key points to remember

  • Log-linear / MaxEnt models: P(y | x) ∝ exp(Σ wi · fi).
  • Discriminative; use arbitrary feature functions.
  • Applications: POS tagging, NER, text classification.
  • CRFs are sequence generalizations of log-linear models.

Q10 — word2vec vs doc2vec

Asked 1 time(May'25 — 8 marks)
How this question appeared in past papers:

(May'25, 8 marks) Explain the concept of doc2vec and its use in generating document embeddings. Discuss the differences between doc2vec and word2vec.

Answer

1. word2vec

  • Developed by Mikolov et al. at Google in 2013.
  • Learns a dense vector representation (embedding) for each WORD.
  • Two architectures:
    • CBOW (Continuous Bag-of-Words): predict target word from context words.
    • Skip-gram: predict context words from target word.
  • Words with similar meanings get similar vectors (e.g., king − man + woman ≈ queen).
  • Typical vector size: 100–300 dimensions.

2. doc2vec (Paragraph Vector)

  • Extension of word2vec by Le and Mikolov (2014).
  • Learns a vector for an ENTIRE document/paragraph, not just words.
  • Two architectures:
    • PV-DM (Distributed Memory): predict word from context + document vector.
    • PV-DBOW (Distributed Bag-of-Words): predict random words in document from document vector.
  • Documents with similar topics get similar vectors.

3. Differences between word2vec and doc2vec

Aspectword2vecdoc2vec
GranularityWord-levelDocument / paragraph level
OutputVector for each wordVector for each document + each word
ArchitecturesCBOW, Skip-gramPV-DM, PV-DBOW
Used forWord similarity, analogiesDocument similarity, classification, clustering
TrainingFaster, simplerSlightly more complex
Handles new docs—Can infer vector for unseen docs

4. Applications of doc2vec

  • Document classification.
  • Document similarity (recommender systems).
  • Sentiment analysis on reviews.
  • Plagiarism detection.
  • News article clustering.

Key points to remember

  • word2vec = word embeddings (CBOW, Skip-gram).
  • doc2vec = document embeddings (PV-DM, PV-DBOW).
  • doc2vec extends word2vec to entire documents.
  • Used for document classification, similarity, clustering.
